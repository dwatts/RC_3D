Resurrection City

Digital Atlas

John Wiebenson, a professor of architecture at the University of Maryland, College Park, created these diagrams that show the purposeful mix of private and public areas created in Resurrection City, some of which were created in part by their design and others happened organically through use. They designed the most public areas like the information center and registration booths, at the western edge of the main pedestrian entrance. Resurrection City's “Main Street” was where reporters, celebrities, federal officials, and visitors could access the encampment when the city was open to the greater public.

Just as in any other “city”, Resurrection City's architects used a classic modernist city planningapproach, with a main street, “publicly” accessible service sites along the main axis, and clusters of different residential “neighborhoods” growing outward according to a loose but clear linear plan, neighborhoods of private living spaces. The story of Resurrection City as a complex but ephemeral settlement built in the midst of all the unquestioned permanence of the Mall's monuments is remarkable - and indiscernible to visitors today.
